# Private Lending vs REITs: Where Should You Put Your Capital?

Both private lending and REITs let individual investors get exposure to real estate without being a landlord. That's where the similarity ends.

The two strategies differ dramatically in how they work, what they pay, how liquid they are, how much work they require, and how they're taxed. This comparison tries to be genuinely useful rather than promotional — there's no universally right answer, and the choice depends on your capital, your time, your tax situation, and your appetite for involvement.

---

## What You're Actually Buying

Before comparing returns, be precise about what each investment is.

**When you buy a REIT (Real Estate Investment Trust):**

You're buying shares in a company that owns real estate — typically commercial real estate like office buildings, apartment complexes, retail centers, industrial warehouses, or specialty properties like data centers and cell towers. The REIT collects rent from tenants, pays operating expenses, and distributes at least 90% of taxable income to shareholders as dividends.

Publicly traded REITs trade on stock exchanges like any stock. Non-traded REITs and private REITs have different liquidity and regulatory profiles.

**When you do private lending:**

You're making a loan secured by a specific piece of real estate. You don't own the property — you're a creditor with a lien. You earn interest on the outstanding principal balance. You get your money back at loan maturity (or when the property sells or refinances).

This distinction matters: a REIT investor participates in the upside and downside of property values and operating performance. A private lender's return is fixed by the interest rate — you don't benefit if the property doubles in value, but you're also protected if it drops modestly (down to your LTV cushion).

## Returns: What Can You Actually Expect?

### REITs

Publicly traded REIT returns come from two sources: dividend yield and share price appreciation (or depreciation).

As of 2026, REIT dividend yields vary widely by sector:
- Industrial/logistics REITs: 3%–5%
- Apartment REITs: 3%–4%
- Mortgage REITs (mREITs): 8%–12% (but with significantly more volatility)
- Specialty REITs (data centers, cell towers): 2%–3%

Long-term total return (dividends plus appreciation) for diversified REIT indices has historically run 8%–12% annually over multi-decade periods — comparable to broad stock market indices. But this requires staying invested through downturns, including periods like 2020 (early COVID crash) and 2022 (aggressive rate hike cycle) when REITs fell 20%–40% before recovering.

Mortgage REITs (mREITs) specifically invest in mortgage-backed securities and real estate loans — arguably the closest REIT equivalent to private lending. Their yields are higher, but they use significant leverage and have historically been volatile, with periodic dividend cuts during credit stress.

### Private Lending

Direct private lending to real estate investors currently yields 9%–14% in interest (annually) plus 1–3 points at origination.

Effective annualized yield — accounting for points, assuming a 12-month average loan duration — typically runs 11%–16% for active lenders with consistent deal flow.

These are gross yields. Net of any defaults (rare in a well-underwritten portfolio, but not zero), dead time between loans (capital not deployed earns nothing), and operating costs, net realized yields typically land in the 9%–13% range for capable lenders.

**The honest comparison:** Private lending yields are typically 2–5 percentage points higher than publicly traded equity REITs on a current yield basis. But the comparison is complicated by leverage, volatility, and tax treatment (covered below).

## Risk: What Could Go Wrong

### REIT Risk

**Market risk.** Publicly traded REITs move with equity markets. During broad market selloffs, REITs often fall even if the underlying real estate is performing fine — you're buying a stock, and stocks get sold during panics. If you needed to sell your REIT in March 2020, you sold at a loss even if all the properties were still collecting rent.

**Interest rate risk.** REITs are particularly sensitive to rising interest rates because they carry significant debt and compete with bonds for yield-seeking capital. The 2022 rate hike cycle was brutal for most REIT categories.

**Operator risk.** REIT performance depends on management quality — their ability to acquire properties at good prices, manage tenants, control costs, and navigate market cycles. Poor management destroys capital.

**Sector risk.** Investing in a single REIT or sector concentrates you in one property type. Office REITs have faced structural headwinds from work-from-home. Retail REITs suffered from e-commerce disruption.

**For mortgage REITs specifically:** Prepayment risk (borrowers pay off early when rates fall, destroying yield), credit risk in their loan books, and the amplifying effect of leverage on losses.

### Private Lending Risk

**Borrower default.** If the borrower stops paying and the deal goes sideways, you initiate foreclosure. This takes months (timeline varies dramatically by state — judicial vs. non-judicial foreclosure), costs legal fees, and ties up your capital. In a properly underwritten loan, you recover principal through the sale — but the process is painful and slow.

**Property value decline.** Your equity cushion protects you down to a certain point. A 65% LTV loan on a property worth $300,000 means the value would need to fall to ~$130,000 (a 57% decline) before you'd lose principal. That doesn't happen often on short-term loans to real estate investors in normal markets — but it can happen in severe downturns.

**Concentration risk.** This is the under-appreciated risk in small private lending portfolios. If you have $300,000 deployed in two loans and one goes sideways, 50% of your portfolio is in workout. Diversification matters.

**Execution risk.** A fix-and-flip depends on the borrower completing the renovation and selling the property within the loan term. Construction cost overruns, contractor problems, permit delays, and slow markets can all extend timelines and create extension requests.

**Illiquidity risk.** Private loans can't be sold on an exchange. If you need your money back before the loan matures, your options are limited and often unfavorable.

**The honest summary:** Private lending risk is more idiosyncratic (specific to each deal and borrower) and less correlated to broader financial markets. REIT risk is more systematic (tied to equity and interest rate markets) and more volatile in the short term. Neither is categorically safer — they're differently dangerous.

## Liquidity: Getting Your Money When You Need It

### REITs

Publicly traded REITs are highly liquid. You can sell shares in seconds during market hours. This is a significant advantage for investors who might need to access capital.

Non-traded REITs and private REITs are illiquid by design, often with lock-up periods of 5–7 years and limited redemption windows.

### Private Lending

Private loans are illiquid. Your capital is committed for the loan term — typically 6–24 months. There's no exchange where you can sell a private loan, though some lenders do sell loans to other private lenders (secondary market) at a discount. Plan on your capital being unavailable until the loan matures.

**The practical implication:** Don't put capital into private lending that you might need within the loan term. Maintain liquid reserves separately.

## Effort Required

### REITs

Near zero ongoing effort. Buy shares, receive dividends, review quarterly reports if you're interested. The REIT's management team handles everything. A REIT is truly passive.

### Private Lending

Active at every stage. Origination requires finding borrowers, evaluating deals, ordering appraisals, working with attorneys on loan documents, coordinating with title companies. Servicing requires tracking payments, communicating with borrowers, monitoring the project. Problem loans require significant time and judgment. Tax time requires careful interest income tracking per loan.

The time investment varies widely. A seasoned private lender with good deal flow and efficient systems might spend 2–5 hours per month managing an active portfolio. Problem loans can demand 20+ hours over their resolution. First-time lenders spend considerably more time per loan while learning.

If your time has high value, factor this into your effective yield calculation. The delta between 11% private lending yield and 5% REIT yield gets smaller when you account for 8 hours a month of management time.

## Tax Treatment

### REITs

REIT dividends are primarily ordinary income, taxed at your marginal rate. However:

- **Qualified dividends:** Some REIT dividends qualify for the lower qualified dividend tax rate
- **Return of capital distributions:** Reduce your cost basis rather than being taxed immediately (tax deferred until sale)
- **Section 199A deduction:** Investors may deduct up to 20% of qualified REIT dividends under the pass-through business income deduction, effectively reducing the tax rate on qualifying distributions

Share price appreciation when sold is taxed as capital gains (long-term rate if held more than one year, short-term if less).

The 199A deduction is a real advantage — it can meaningfully reduce the effective tax rate on REIT dividend income for investors who qualify.

### Private Lending

Interest income is taxed as ordinary income, period. No preferential rate, no depreciation offset, no 199A deduction (in most cases for individual lenders), no capital gains treatment.

If your lending qualifies as a business (Schedule C), you may owe self-employment tax (15.3%) on net earnings, on top of ordinary income tax.

**The tax conclusion:** For high-income investors in the highest tax brackets, REITs often have a tax efficiency advantage over private lending, especially when accounting for the 199A deduction and return-of-capital treatment. This can meaningfully narrow the pre-tax yield difference.

## Who Should Choose Which

**Private lending makes more sense if:**
- You have sufficient capital to deploy across 3+ loans simultaneously
- You have deal flow access (network in real estate investing communities)
- You want higher yields and are willing to do the work
- Your tax situation doesn't make ordinary income particularly punishing
- You understand real estate well enough to evaluate collateral

**REITs make more sense if:**
- You want genuine passivity
- You may need to access capital within a few years
- You're in a high tax bracket where ordinary income treatment is costly
- Your capital base is too small to diversify across private loans
- You want diversification across many properties and geographies

**Neither is universally better.** Many sophisticated investors hold both — using REITs for liquid, diversified real estate exposure and private lending for higher-yield fixed-income-like returns. The combination can make sense as part of a broader portfolio.

# 5 Mistakes New Private Lenders Make (And How to Avoid Them)

Private lending looks deceptively simple from the outside. You have capital; someone needs a loan; you charge interest; everyone wins. And most of the time, that's exactly how it works.

But private lending mistakes are expensive — sometimes five or six figures of expensive. And the painful part is that almost every significant loss in private lending traces back to one of the same few errors. Experienced lenders recognize these patterns immediately. New lenders discover them through expensive tuition.

This guide exists to make that tuition cheaper.

---

## Mistake #1: Lending on Bad (or Unverified) Collateral

Your loan is secured by real property. That means the property is the last line of defense between you and a loss. If the borrower defaults, you foreclose and sell the collateral to recover your principal. Everything in private lending depends on this working.

It only works if the collateral is actually worth what you think it's worth.

**How new lenders get this wrong:**

They accept the borrower's valuation. The borrower says the property will be worth $300,000 after renovation, so the lender structures a $200,000 loan against that number — a "comfortable" 67% LTV. Except the borrower's ARV was based on a listing they saw six months ago in a different neighborhood. The real comparable sales point to $225,000. Now the lender has a $200,000 loan on a $225,000 property — 89% LTV — and almost no cushion.

**Other collateral mistakes:**

*Unusual property types.* A single-family house in a normal neighborhood is easy to sell. A renovated church-turned-loft in a rural area with one stoplight is not. If your collateral is unusual, illiquid, or in a thin market, you need to be much more conservative on LTV — or decline entirely.

*Deferred maintenance and unknown problems.* You approved the loan based on the seller's listing photos. Nobody walked the property. The basement has been flooding for three years and the foundation has shifted. The renovation budget is about to triple.

*Environmental issues.* Underground oil tanks, lead paint, asbestos, mold, prior drug manufacturing — all can make a property virtually unsellable. A simple environmental questionnaire at origination catches most of this.

*Unknown liens.* You're confident you're in first lien position. You didn't order a title search. There's a $40,000 tax lien from three years ago sitting ahead of you.

**How to protect yourself:**

Order a broker price opinion (BPO) or appraisal from someone with no connection to the borrower. Walk the property, or send someone you trust. Require a lender's title insurance policy at closing — this protects you against undiscovered liens and title defects. Include a site inspection checklist in your underwriting process.

For fix-and-flip loans specifically, evaluate the ARV conservatively. Use the median comparable sale in the area, not the high. Apply a 5–10% haircut to account for market softness and selling costs. Then check your LTV against that conservative number.

## Mistake #2: Concentration Risk (All Your Eggs in One Basket)

This mistake is invisible until it isn't.

A new lender has $250,000 available. They find a deal they like and deploy the full amount into a single $250,000 loan. It's a good deal — conservative LTV, experienced borrower, liquid collateral. Everything checks out.

The borrower runs into permit delays. The project extends from 12 months to 18 months. Then 22 months. The lender needs capital to help a family member. The deal is technically performing — payments are still coming — but the lender can't access the capital.

That's a best-case concentration scenario. Worst case: the single loan goes into default. Now 100% of your private lending portfolio is in workout, consuming time, legal fees, and emotional energy, while your capital is frozen for however long foreclosure takes.

**The math of concentration:**

With $250,000 across 5 loans at $50,000 each, a default on one loan means 20% of your portfolio is in problem territory. Painful, but manageable. The other 4 loans continue generating income. Your cash flow carries the workout costs.

With $250,000 in one loan, one default means 100% of your portfolio is frozen. There's no cash flow from other loans to fund legal costs. You're writing checks to an attorney while waiting months for a foreclosure to resolve.

**The uncomfortable reality for new lenders:**

Starting out, diversification is hard. You may not have enough capital to make multiple loans simultaneously, or you may not have enough deal flow to deploy into multiple deals at once.

If that's your situation, recognize the concentration risk explicitly and size your first loan accordingly. Consider starting smaller — a $75,000 first loan rather than $200,000 — to preserve capital for a second deal and maintain a meaningful reserve.

**Reserve capital is not optional:**

Keep 10–15% of your private lending capital in liquid reserves. This covers legal fees if a loan goes into workout, carries you through a month or two of missed payments while you work things out, and gives you optionality. Deploying every dollar into loans is how lenders end up in financial stress when a single problem emerges.

## Mistake #3: Inadequate Documentation

"We kept it simple" is a phrase that shows up a lot in private lending disputes. Simple documentation feels fine when everything is going well. When things go sideways, "simple" becomes "unenforceable."

**The documents that matter:**

*Promissory note.* This is the borrower's written promise to repay. It needs to specify: principal amount, interest rate, payment schedule, maturity date, what constitutes default, and the lender's remedies upon default. A note downloaded from a template site and filled in with the loan terms is not a properly prepared note — it may contain errors, miss state-specific requirements, or omit provisions that courts in your state require.

*Deed of trust or mortgage.* This creates the lien against the property. It must be properly prepared and recorded with the county recorder's office to be enforceable. If it's not recorded, you don't have a security interest — you have an unsecured personal loan regardless of what the note says.

*Personal guarantee.* If you're lending to an LLC, a personal guarantee from the individual behind the LLC gives you a second recovery path. Without it, your recourse in a default is limited to the collateral. With it, you can pursue the borrower personally if the collateral doesn't cover the balance.

*Title insurance.* A lender's title policy protects you against undiscovered title defects and prior liens. It costs a few hundred dollars at closing. Not having it is a gamble with your principal.

*Proof of insurance.* The borrower must maintain property insurance, with you named as additional insured and loss payee. If the property burns down and there's no insurance, you have a lien on an empty lot worth a fraction of the loan amount.

**The "friend or family" problem:**

The documentation problem is most acute in loans to people you know. The borrower is your brother-in-law, your college roommate, your longtime business partner. Asking for a properly documented loan feels awkward — like you don't trust them.

Here's the reframe: documentation protects the relationship, not just the money. If the loan goes sideways, ambiguity about terms creates disputes that destroy relationships. A properly documented loan — even with someone you completely trust — means both parties have the same understanding of the terms. There's no dispute about whether the extension request was agreed to, whether there's a prepayment penalty, or when exactly the loan matures.

Document every loan the same way, regardless of who the borrower is. Tell your borrower exactly this: "I document all my loans the same way. It protects both of us."

**Work with a real estate attorney.** Document templates are a starting point, not an endpoint. Have an attorney in your state review and customize your document package for your loan types and jurisdiction. Spend $500 on this upfront. The alternative is spending $5,000–$15,000 trying to enforce a poorly drafted note in court.

## Mistake #4: Ignoring Legal and Regulatory Requirements

Private lending operates within a legal framework that varies by state, loan type, borrower type, and loan volume. Ignoring this framework is how lenders end up with unenforceable loans, regulatory penalties, or criminal liability.

**The key legal areas new lenders miss:**

*Usury laws.* Every state limits the maximum interest rate on loans. The limits differ based on whether the loan is to a consumer or business borrower, the loan amount, and the lender's license status. Charging a rate above the legal limit can void the loan's interest provisions — meaning you may only be entitled to recover principal, not interest — and can trigger civil or criminal penalties.

Most private real estate lending (to business entities, for business purposes) is exempt from the consumer usury protections that would otherwise apply. But "most" isn't "all." Know the rules in your state.

*Licensing requirements.* Many states require mortgage broker or lender licenses for people who originate or fund real estate loans, even if they're lending their own money. The thresholds and exemptions vary. In some states, making more than a certain number of loans per year triggers licensing requirements. In others, lending to business entities is exempt.

This isn't something to google your way to a conclusion on. Consult a real estate attorney in your state who knows lending law, and get a clear answer before you make your first loan.

*Securities law.* If you eventually raise capital from other investors to fund loans, you're potentially selling securities. This triggers SEC and state securities regulations that are well beyond the scope of this article. Do not raise outside capital without securities law counsel.

*Fair lending laws.* Even private lenders must comply with fair lending requirements. Declining a loan based on the borrower's race, religion, national origin, or other protected characteristics is illegal. Make sure your lending criteria are documented, objective, and applied consistently.

**The practical minimum:** Before you make your first loan, have a 60-minute conversation with a real estate attorney in your state. Pay for the consultation. Come prepared with your business plan, intended loan types, expected volume, and questions about licensing, usury limits, and required documentation. This is the single most valuable hour you'll spend as a new private lender.

## Mistake #5: Poor Tracking and Record-Keeping

This mistake doesn't cause a loss on any single loan — it's death by a thousand cuts, and it surfaces most painfully at tax time.

**How the tracking problem unfolds:**

Month 1: One loan, easy. You know the balance, the rate, the payment date.

Month 6: Three active loans. You think you remember who owes what, but you're not 100% sure the last payment from Borrower B cleared. Your spreadsheet hasn't been updated in 6 weeks.

Month 12: Five loans. One is in a 90-day extension you verbally agreed to but never documented. Another borrower made an overpayment and you're not sure how to apply it to principal vs. interest. Tax time is approaching and your accountant needs per-loan interest totals. You spend three days reconciling spreadsheets and bank statements.

Month 18: One loan went to default. You're trying to document your claim in a foreclosure proceeding. Your records are incomplete. The attorney asks for the full payment history and you don't have a clean one.

**The compounding problem:**

Private loans have nuance. Payments split between principal and interest differently every month (for amortizing loans). Partial payments create weird edge cases. Overpayments need to be applied correctly. Extensions change the maturity date and sometimes the rate. Late fees need to be tracked separately from interest.

A spreadsheet can theoretically handle all of this — but only if it's maintained fastidiously, updated with every payment, and built correctly in the first place. Most spreadsheets that start simple acquire complexity through patches and workarounds. The result is a system nobody fully trusts.

**What good tracking looks like:**

A proper loan management system gives you, at any moment:
- The outstanding principal balance on every loan
- Interest accrued but not yet paid
- A full payment history per loan (with principal/interest split)
- Upcoming payment dates
- Year-to-date interest received per borrower
- Any loans with missed payments or alerts

If you can't produce per-loan interest totals at year end, your accountant has to reconstruct them — which costs you money in accounting fees. If you filed 1098s incorrectly because your numbers were off, that's an IRS problem. If you're ever audited, incomplete records create exposure that good records would eliminate.

## The Pattern Behind These Mistakes

Look at these five mistakes together and you'll notice a theme: most of them come from treating private lending as a casual activity rather than a business.

Casual activities don't need proper legal structure, detailed documentation, collateral verification, or systematic record-keeping. Businesses do.

Private lending is a business. You are extending credit secured by real property, subject to state and federal law, generating taxable income that must be reported accurately, and managing risk that, if ignored, can cost you significant capital.

The infrastructure you build at the beginning — legal documents, underwriting discipline, tracking systems — protects you from mistakes that would otherwise cost 10–50 times what the infrastructure costs to build.

Build it right from the start.

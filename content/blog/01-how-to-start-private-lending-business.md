# How to Start a Private Lending Business: A Practical Guide

Private lending is one of the simplest high-yield investment structures available to individual investors — and one of the most misunderstood. You lend money secured by real estate, collect interest monthly, and get your principal back when the loan is repaid or the property sells. No tenants, no toilets, no stock market volatility.

But "simple" doesn't mean "easy." Most people who fail at private lending don't fail because they picked the wrong interest rate. They fail because they skipped the legal foundation, lent to the wrong people, or managed their loans out of a spreadsheet until things got complicated.

This guide covers what actually matters — not theory, but the practical decisions you'll face in your first 12 months.

---

## What Private Lending Actually Is

Private lending (also called hard money lending at the business scale) means lending your own capital — or capital you've raised from others — directly to borrowers, secured by real property. You are the bank.

The borrowers are typically real estate investors: fix-and-flip operators, landlords acquiring rentals, developers doing ground-up construction. They come to private lenders because they can't or won't wait for a conventional mortgage — the property needs too much work, the timeline is too short, or they want to close in days rather than months.

As the lender, you get:
- A promissory note (the borrower's legal obligation to repay)
- A recorded deed of trust or mortgage lien on the property
- A monthly interest payment (or accrued interest, depending on structure)
- Your principal back at loan maturity

What you don't get: appreciation, equity upside, or passive ownership. You are a creditor, not an investor in the property itself.

## Step 1: Choose Your Legal Structure

Before you lend a single dollar, decide how you'll hold the business.

**Sole Proprietorship / Personal Lending**

The simplest option: you lend money in your own name. Legally valid in most states. No entity formation required. The downside is that your personal assets are exposed if a borrower sues you or a deal goes sideways. For your first one or two loans to people you know well, this is fine. For anything beyond that, it's risky.

**LLC (Most Common Choice)**

An LLC lets you lend through a separate legal entity, which creates a liability shield between your personal assets and any business disputes. A single-member LLC taxed as a disregarded entity is the most common setup for solo private lenders — you get liability protection with minimal administrative overhead.

Formation costs run $50–$500 depending on your state. You'll need:
- Articles of Organization filed with your state
- An Operating Agreement (even for a single-member LLC — banks and title companies will ask for it)
- A separate business bank account
- An EIN from the IRS

**Series LLC or Multiple LLCs**

Some lenders hold each loan in a separate entity to isolate risk. This is more complexity than most micro-lenders need early on, but worth knowing about as your portfolio grows.

**Consult an attorney.** This guide gives you the framework, not legal advice. A one-hour consultation with a real estate attorney in your state is worth every penny before you start.

## Step 2: Know Your State's Usury Laws

Every state has usury laws that cap interest rates on loans. These vary widely — some states have no practical cap for business loans; others are strict. California, for example, has different rules depending on whether the borrower is a consumer or a business entity.

The good news: most private lending is done to business borrowers (real estate investors using business entities), which often exempts the loan from consumer usury protections. But this isn't universal.

Key questions to answer with your attorney:
- Does your state require a lending license for your loan types and volumes?
- What's the maximum allowable rate for business-purpose loans in your state?
- Do you need to register as a mortgage lender or broker?

Most small-scale private lenders (fewer than a handful of loans per year, lending to business entities secured by real property) fly well under the licensing threshold. But get a clear answer for your specific state before you proceed.

## Step 3: Determine Your Capital Base

How much do you actually have available to lend? This seems obvious, but it requires honest accounting.

**Working capital vs. total assets**

Don't count your emergency fund, retirement accounts, or money you'd need within 12 months. Private loans are illiquid. A 12-month loan to a fix-and-flip investor means your capital is locked up for at least that long, potentially longer if the project runs over.

**Realistic deployment targets**

If you have $200,000 available, a single loan deploys it all — which creates concentration risk (more on this later). A more conservative approach might be 3–5 loans of $40,000–$70,000 each, depending on your market.

**Reserve capital**

Keep 10–15% of your portfolio value in liquid reserves. If a borrower misses a payment and you need to carry the loan for a few months while you work it out, you don't want to be personally stressed about cash flow.

**Raising capital from others**

Some lenders eventually raise capital from friends, family, or accredited investors to grow their loan book. This introduces securities law considerations — selling investment opportunities to third parties can trigger SEC or state securities regulations. This is a much bigger legal topic and well beyond the scope of starting out. Focus on your own capital first.

## Step 4: Define Your Lending Criteria

Before you evaluate your first deal, write down what you will and won't lend on. This is your underwriting policy. Having it in writing protects you from "deal fever" — the emotional pressure to make a loan work when it shouldn't.

**Decide on:**

*Property types.* Single-family residential is the most common starting point. It's the easiest to value, the most liquid collateral, and the simplest to foreclose on if necessary. Commercial, multi-family, and land all carry more complexity.

*Maximum LTV.* This is your loan-to-value ratio — how much you'll lend relative to the property's value. Most private lenders start at 65–70% LTV on residential fix-and-flip (meaning if a property is worth $200,000, you'd lend up to $130,000–$140,000). Lower LTV means more equity cushion protecting you.

*Loan term.* Most private loans run 6–24 months. Longer terms give you steady income but lock up capital longer. Shorter terms (6–12 months) are common for fix-and-flip.

*Minimum deal size.* There's a floor below which the paperwork and legal cost isn't worth it. Many lenders won't go below $25,000–$50,000.

*Geographic focus.* Stick to markets you understand. It's much easier to evaluate a deal in your own city than somewhere you've never been.

## Step 5: Find Borrowers

This is where most new lenders get stuck. Your capital is ready; you don't know who to lend to.

**Real estate investor meetups**

Every mid-sized city has REI meetups — local real estate investor groups that meet monthly. These are your people. Fix-and-flip operators, landlords, and developers attend these regularly and actively look for private money. Show up, introduce yourself as a private lender, and be honest about your criteria. Don't sell; just let people know you exist.

**Hard money lender networks**

Local hard money companies sometimes co-lend or refer deals they can't take. Building a relationship with established lenders in your market can produce deal flow once they trust you.

**Referrals from real estate professionals**

Real estate agents, title company reps, and attorneys who work with investors all see deals constantly. Let your professional network know what you do.

**Online communities**

Bigger Pockets, local Facebook groups for real estate investors, and LinkedIn are all valid channels. Don't lead with "I'm a lender" — engage genuinely, and your activity will surface the right conversations.

**Patience is required.** Most new private lenders get their first deal from someone they already know. The referral network takes 6–12 months to build from scratch. This is normal.

## Step 6: Evaluate Deals

For every deal you consider, you need to answer four questions:

1. **What is the property worth today?** Get a broker price opinion (BPO) or order an appraisal. Don't take the borrower's number at face value.

2. **What will it be worth after repairs (ARV)?** For fix-and-flip loans, the "after repair value" is the number that matters at loan maturity. Use comparable sales in the same neighborhood, not the borrower's optimistic projection.

3. **Does the LTV work?** Calculate your loan against current value AND against ARV. You want a cushion in both.

4. **Can the borrower execute?** Track record matters more than credit score in private lending. Has this person successfully completed similar projects? Do they have a realistic budget and timeline?

## Step 7: Get the Right Documents

Private lending paperwork is non-negotiable. Don't let a borrower talk you into "keeping it simple." Simple documentation creates complex legal problems.

**The essential documents:**

*Promissory Note.* The borrower's written promise to repay under specific terms — principal, interest rate, payment schedule, maturity date, and what happens in default. Prepared by an attorney; not downloaded from a template site.

*Deed of Trust or Mortgage.* The lien on the property. This is what secures your loan — it gives you the right to foreclose if the borrower doesn't pay. Must be recorded with the county recorder's office.

*Loan Agreement.* Spells out fees, prepayment terms, extension options, and any specific conditions.

*Title Insurance.* A lender's title insurance policy protects you if there's a title defect (a lien you didn't know about, ownership dispute, etc.). Always require it.

*Property Insurance.* The borrower must maintain insurance on the property, with you named as an additional insured. Get a certificate.

*Personal Guarantee.* For loans to LLCs, a personal guarantee from the individual(s) behind the entity gives you a second recovery path if the deal goes bad.

Work with a real estate attorney in your state to create a document package appropriate for your loan types. The cost is a few hundred dollars and it's the best money you'll spend.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Lending to people, not deals.** The deal has to work on paper even if the borrower turns out to be unreliable. Friendship doesn't repay loans.

**Skipping the title search.** You could unknowingly be in second or third lien position behind tax liens or other debts you didn't know existed. Title insurance and a proper title search prevent this.

**Letting the borrower pressure your valuation.** "The ARV is definitely $400,000" is something every borrower believes. Verify it yourself with independent comps.

**No reserve for problem loans.** Even good lenders have borrowers who go quiet. If you need to hire an attorney or carry a loan through a workout, that costs money. Plan for it.

**Poor documentation.** A handshake loan to a brother-in-law is fine until it isn't. Document everything, every time.

**Letting loans run without tracking.** When you have multiple loans active, tracking payment status, outstanding balances, and tax records in your head — or scattered across spreadsheets — invites errors. ## Getting Started: A Realistic Timeline

**Month 1–2:** Legal setup, document templates, underwriting criteria. No loans yet.

**Month 2–4:** Networking, meet investors, evaluate deals without committing. Learn the market.

**Month 4–6:** First loan. Start small. Get comfortable with the documentation and servicing process.

**Month 6–12:** Second and third loan. Refine your criteria based on what you've learned. Build your referral network.

Private lending isn't a get-rich-quick strategy — it's a business. Built carefully, with proper legal structure, disciplined underwriting, and good documentation, it generates consistent, secured income that compounds year over year.

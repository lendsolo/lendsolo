# How to Structure a Hard Money Loan: Terms, Docs, and Protections

A hard money loan that's structured well protects your capital on day one and gives you clear options if things go sideways on day 180. A poorly structured one might feel fine at closing, but it creates ambiguity around defaults, gaps in your collateral position, and servicing headaches that eat into your returns.

Structuring isn't complicated—it's a finite set of decisions around term, rate, payment schedule, LTV, and documentation. This guide walks through each one, plus the documents and protections that separate professional lenders from people who are just hoping for the best.

## The Core Terms Every Hard Money Loan Needs

Before you get into documents and legal protections, you need to nail down the economic terms. These are the numbers and conditions that define the deal. Every term sheet and promissory note should address all of them explicitly.

### Principal Amount

This is the total amount you're lending. Straightforward—but make sure the principal reflects the actual disbursement schedule. On a fix-and-flip loan with construction draws, you might commit $200,000 total but only fund $140,000 at closing, with the remaining $60,000 released in draws as work is completed. Your documents should specify both the total commitment and the initial funding amount.

### Interest Rate

Most hard money loans carry fixed rates between 9% and 14%, depending on the deal's risk profile, the borrower's experience, and current market conditions. Some lenders use variable rates tied to the prime rate or SOFR, but fixed is standard in the private lending space—borrowers want predictability, and frankly, so do you.

Stick with simple annual interest calculated on the actual outstanding principal. Exotic calculation methods create confusion and potential legal risk.

### Term Length

Match the term to the borrower's actual business plan—with a buffer:

- **Fix-and-flip**: 6–12 months is standard. If the borrower says the rehab will take 4 months and the sale another 2, a 9-month term gives reasonable cushion. A 6-month term on a project with a 5-month timeline is asking for a maturity default.
- **Bridge loans**: 12–18 months is typical. The borrower is bridging to a refinance, a sale, or a stabilization event that takes longer than a quick flip.
- **Stabilized rental or longer-term holds**: 24–60 months. These are less common for hard money but do exist, especially for borrowers building a portfolio who need time to hit the debt-service-coverage ratios that conventional lenders require.

Setting the term too short is one of the most common structuring mistakes. When a loan matures and the borrower can't pay off, your options are limited to extending (on your terms, hopefully) or foreclosing. Neither is ideal. Give the business plan room to breathe.

### Origination Fee (Points)

Standard range: 1–3 points (1–3% of the principal amount). One point on a $250,000 loan is $2,500. Points are typically collected at closing, deducted from the loan proceeds.

Points serve two purposes: they compensate you for the work of underwriting and closing the deal, and they boost your overall yield. A 12-month loan at 12% interest with 2 points has an effective annualized yield of about 14%. On a 6-month loan at the same rate, those 2 points effectively annualize to 16%+, because you're earning the same fee over a shorter period.

### Payment Structure

You have two basic options here: interest-only payments or fully amortizing payments. More on this in the next section.

### Prepayment Terms

Decide whether the borrower can pay off early and under what conditions. Options include no prepayment penalty (most common for short-term fix-and-flip), a minimum interest guarantee (e.g., "borrower owes a minimum of 3 months' interest regardless of when the loan is repaid"), or a sliding penalty (e.g., 2% in year one, 1% in year two, none after that).

A minimum interest guarantee is common and reasonable for short-term loans. Without one, a borrower who pays off a 12-month loan in 60 days leaves you with barely enough interest to cover your origination costs.

### Default Provisions

Specify exactly what constitutes a default: missed payment (how many days late?), failure to maintain insurance, failure to pay property taxes, violation of loan covenants, bankruptcy filing, material misrepresentation. Then specify the consequences: default interest rate (typically 3–5% above the note rate), acceleration of the full balance, lender's right to foreclose.

Don't leave defaults vague. "Material breach of the loan agreement" is not a default provision—it's an invitation to a legal argument.

## Interest-Only vs. Fully Amortizing: When to Use Each

This is one of the most consequential structural decisions, and it depends entirely on the loan's purpose and timeline.

### Interest-Only

Interest-only is the standard payment structure for short-term hard money loans (under 24 months). The borrower pays only the interest each month—no principal reduction—and owes the full principal at maturity.

Why this works for short-term deals: the borrower's exit strategy is to sell the property or refinance into a long-term loan. They don't need to pay down principal because the principal will be repaid in full when the exit event happens. Interest-only payments keep monthly costs low, which preserves the borrower's cash for the rehab or operating expenses.

**Example**: $250,000 loan at 12% interest-only. Monthly payment: $2,500. The borrower pays $2,500/month for the life of the loan and owes $250,000 at maturity.

### Fully Amortizing

Fully amortizing loans spread principal repayment across the life of the loan, so each monthly payment includes both interest and principal. The balance declines over time.

Use this structure for longer-term loans (24+ months) where the borrower is holding the property and generating rental income. The principal paydown reduces your exposure over time, and the higher monthly payments are supported by the property's cash flow.

**Example**: $250,000 loan at 12% over 60 months, fully amortizing. Monthly payment: $5,561. After 24 months, the remaining balance is approximately $197,000—you've reduced your exposure by over $53,000.

### The Hybrid Option

Some lenders use a hybrid structure: interest-only for a set period (say, 6–12 months during a renovation), then converting to fully amortizing for the remainder. This works well for BRRRR-strategy borrowers (buy, rehab, rent, refinance, repeat) who need low payments during the rehab phase but will have rental income to support higher payments once the property is stabilized.

## Setting the Right LTV Ratio

Loan-to-value ratio is your primary risk control. It determines how much equity cushion sits between your loan and potential loss. Getting this right is more important than getting the interest rate right—a great rate on a loan with thin collateral coverage is a bad deal.

### LTV vs. LTARV

There are two ways to measure LTV in hard money lending:

- **As-is LTV**: Loan amount divided by the property's current market value. Used for stabilized properties or bridge loans where no significant improvements are planned.
- **LTV based on After-Repair Value (ARV)**: Loan amount divided by the property's projected value after renovations. Standard for fix-and-flip loans where the borrower is adding value through rehab.

These produce very different numbers. A property worth $150,000 as-is that will be worth $250,000 after a $60,000 rehab: if you lend $175,000, your as-is LTV is 117%—terrible. Your ARV LTV is 70%—solid.

### Typical LTV Ranges

Standard ranges: 65–75% of ARV for fix-and-flip deals, 60–75% of as-is value for stabilized or bridge loans. At 70% ARV, you have a 30% equity cushion—the property's value would have to decline 30% before your principal is at risk.

### Why 75% ARV Is the Ceiling

Going above 75% ARV changes your risk profile dramatically. Here's the math:

At 70% ARV on a $250,000 property, you've lent $175,000. If the borrower defaults and you foreclose, you need to recover $175,000 plus foreclosure costs (let's say $15,000). That's $190,000, which means you can take a 24% haircut on the property's value and still break even.

At 85% ARV, you've lent $212,500. Add $15,000 in foreclosure costs, and you need to recover $227,500. Now you can only absorb a 9% decline. One bad appraisal, one soft market, or one incomplete rehab and you're upside down.

The difference between 70% and 85% ARV isn't 15 percentage points of risk—it's the difference between a loan that can survive a rough exit and one that can't.

### Evaluating Collateral

Don't rely solely on the borrower's ARV estimate. Get an independent appraisal or BPO (broker price opinion) from someone who knows the local market. For fix-and-flip loans, review the rehab budget line by line and compare it against recent comparable renovations. Verify comparable sales yourself—the borrower's comps are always optimistic.

## Essential Documents for Every Private Loan

You can have perfect terms and still lose money if your documentation is incomplete. These are the non-negotiable documents for every private real estate loan:

### Promissory Note

The promissory note is the document that creates the debt. It specifies the principal amount, interest rate, payment schedule, maturity date, default provisions, and all other economic terms. Without a valid promissory note, you don't have an enforceable debt—you have a handshake.

The note should be signed by the borrower (and any guarantors) and notarized. Keep the original in a secure location.

### Deed of Trust (or Mortgage)

The deed of trust is what secures the debt against the real property. It gives you the right to foreclose if the borrower defaults. In deed-of-trust states (like Texas, California, and Virginia), this document is filed with the county recorder and creates your lien on the property.

**Critical**: the deed of trust must be recorded with the county to establish lien priority. An unrecorded deed of trust gives you zero protection against subsequent liens. Record it the same day as closing, every single time.

### Personal Guarantee

If the borrower is an entity (LLC, corporation, partnership), the entity's assets are the only thing backing the loan—unless you have a personal guarantee. A personal guarantee from the entity's principals means that if the entity can't repay, the individuals behind it are personally liable.

This is especially important for single-asset LLCs, which are common in real estate. An LLC that owns one property and has no other assets gives you nothing beyond the property itself if things go wrong. A personal guarantee from the borrower means you can pursue their other assets if the property's value doesn't cover the debt.

### Hazard Insurance

Require the borrower to carry hazard insurance (property insurance) covering fire, storm, and other damage, with you named as the **loss payee** or **mortgagee** on the policy. This means if the property is damaged or destroyed, the insurance payout comes to you first.

Get proof of insurance before funding. Set up an alert or calendar reminder to verify renewal before the policy lapses. An uninsured property that burns down is a total loss for everyone.

### Title Insurance

A lender's title insurance policy protects you against defects in the title that weren't revealed by the title search—forged deeds, undisclosed liens, recording errors, missing heirs. The cost is a one-time premium at closing, and it's one of the highest-value protections you can buy.

### Construction Draw Schedule (If Applicable)

For fix-and-flip and renovation loans where you're funding the rehab, include a draw schedule that specifies how and when construction funds will be released. Typical structure: the borrower completes a phase of work, you (or your inspector) verify completion, and then you release the draw. Never front-load construction funds—the borrower should have their own capital at risk in the early stages of the project.

## Protecting Yourself as the Lender

Beyond the core documents, there are structural protections that experienced lenders build into every deal:

### Insurance Requirements

Beyond basic hazard insurance, consider requiring flood insurance (if the property is in a flood zone—check FEMA maps), builder's risk insurance (for heavy renovation projects), and general liability insurance if the property is occupied or under construction. Every policy should name you as an additional insured or loss payee.

### Inspection Rights

Your loan agreement should give you the right to inspect the property at any time with reasonable notice (24–48 hours). For construction loans, tie draw releases to satisfactory inspections. You should never be in a position where you don't know the current condition of your collateral.

### Cross-Default and Assignment Clauses

If you make multiple loans to the same borrower, include a cross-default clause: a default on any one loan constitutes a default on all of them. Also make sure your documents allow you to assign (sell) the loan to another party without borrower consent—if you ever need liquidity, clean assignment rights make a loan far easier to sell on the secondary market.

### Property Tax and Insurance Escrow

On loans over 12 months, consider requiring escrow for property taxes and insurance. This protects you from the common scenario where a borrower stops paying taxes and a tax lien jumps ahead of your deed of trust in priority.

## Common Structuring Mistakes

These are the mistakes I see most often—and every one of them is avoidable:

### Not Requiring a Personal Guarantee on Entity Loans

This is probably the single most common mistake among newer private lenders. The borrower forms a fresh LLC, buys a property through it, and the lender lends to the LLC without a personal guarantee. If the deal goes bad, the LLC files bankruptcy, and the lender's only recourse is the property itself—which, if the market has softened or the rehab isn't done, may not cover the debt.

Always get a personal guarantee from the principals. If the borrower refuses, that itself is a red flag—it may mean they don't have confidence in their own deal.

### Setting the Term Too Short

A 6-month term on a project that realistically needs 8 months to renovate and sell puts you in a maturity default scenario almost by design. The borrower isn't actually defaulting on the business plan—they're defaulting on an unrealistic timeline that the lender imposed.

Build in at least 3 months of cushion beyond the borrower's projected timeline. On a fix-and-flip where the borrower estimates 6 months for rehab plus sale, use a 9–12 month term.

### Not Including Extension Provisions

Even with a reasonable term, projects run long. Include a clear extension provision: the borrower can extend for 3–6 months by paying an extension fee (typically 0.5–1 point) and remaining current on payments. This gives both parties a structured way to handle delays without triggering a default.

Without an extension provision, a maturity default forces you to choose between informally extending the loan (with no additional compensation and weakened legal position) or initiating foreclosure on a borrower who's otherwise performing. Neither is a good option.

### Forgetting About Property Taxes and Insurance

Your deed of trust means nothing if a property tax lien takes senior position. And a property without hazard insurance is one fire away from a total loss. Build monitoring systems for both: require proof of insurance annually (or more frequently), verify tax payments quarterly, and consider requiring escrow on longer-term loans.

Make tax and insurance verification part of your quarterly portfolio review.

## Conclusion

Every term, document, and protection you put in place at closing is one less problem you have to solve later. Start with clean terms that match the business plan, get every document executed and recorded, and build in protections that give you options when things don't go according to plan.

The lenders who last in this business aren't the ones who find the best deals. They're the ones who structure every deal as if it might go wrong, and then are pleasantly surprised when it doesn't.

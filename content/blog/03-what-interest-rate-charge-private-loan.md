# What Interest Rate Should I Charge on a Private Loan?

This is the question every new private lender asks immediately after "is this legal?" And it's the right question — but it doesn't have a single answer. The rate you charge should reflect the risk of the specific deal in front of you, the market you're operating in, and the minimum return that makes the loan worth your while.

This guide gives you a framework to arrive at the right number, not a table you can copy blindly.

---

## What the Market Looks Like Right Now

Private loan rates aren't published in the Wall Street Journal. They're negotiated deal by deal, in local markets, between lenders who know their turf and borrowers who know their options.

That said, there are rough market ranges as of 2026:

**Residential Fix-and-Flip**
- Rate range: 10%–14% annually
- Typical term: 6–18 months
- LTV: 65%–75% of ARV (after repair value)
- Points: 1–3% origination fee

**Short-Term Bridge Loans (Stabilized Property)**
- Rate range: 9%–13% annually
- Typical term: 6–24 months
- LTV: 60%–70% of current value
- Points: 1–2%

**Ground-Up Construction**
- Rate range: 11%–15%+ annually
- Typical term: 12–24 months
- LTV: 60%–65% of projected completed value
- Points: 2–4%
- Note: These are higher risk. The collateral doesn't fully exist yet.

**Rental Property Acquisition (DSCR Loans)**
- Rate range: 8%–12% annually
- Typical term: 12–36 months
- LTV: 65%–75%

**Loans to Known, Experienced Borrowers**
- Often 1–3 points below market depending on relationship, track record, and deal quality

These are ranges, not rules. Rates in high-cost coastal markets (California, New York) often run higher than in Midwest markets. Lender supply and demand in your specific geography matters.

## The Five Factors That Drive Your Rate

Think of rate-setting as a starting point adjusted up or down by specific risk factors.

### 1. Loan-to-Value Ratio (LTV)

LTV is the single most important factor in private loan pricing. It determines how much equity cushion exists between you and a loss.

At 50% LTV on a $300,000 property, your $150,000 loan is protected by $150,000 in equity. The property would have to lose more than half its value before you're at risk of loss — which almost never happens in a short-term loan to a real estate investor.

At 80% LTV on the same property, your $240,000 loan has only $60,000 in equity cushion — and that cushion shrinks further when you account for selling costs, carrying costs during foreclosure, and any deferred maintenance.

**General pricing adjustment for LTV:**
- Below 55% LTV: Consider pricing at the low end of market or offering a rate incentive
- 55–65% LTV: Market rate, minimal adjustment
- 65–75% LTV: Standard range, no adjustment needed if deal is otherwise sound
- Above 75% LTV: Significant risk premium required, or decline the loan

### 2. Borrower Experience and Track Record

A borrower with 20 completed fix-and-flip projects in your market is a fundamentally different credit risk than someone doing their first deal.

Experienced operators:
- Know what renovations actually cost (and don't underestimate by 40%)
- Have contractor relationships and can execute on schedule
- Understand the exit — they know how to price and sell
- Have skin in the game in the form of reputation in the local investor community

Pricing adjustment: 1–2 percentage points lower for proven, experienced operators. First-time borrowers should be priced at the higher end of range, and you should be more conservative on LTV.

### 3. Property Type and Liquidity

Single-family residential in a suburban market is the most liquid collateral in private lending. If you had to foreclose and sell, you'd have buyers. That liquidity is worth something in your rate calculation.

As collateral gets less liquid, the rate goes up:
- **SFR in suburban or urban market:** Standard rate
- **Multi-family (2–4 units):** Slight premium
- **Multi-family (5+ units):** Larger premium, requires more specialized buyer pool
- **Commercial (retail, office, industrial):** Significant premium, can take much longer to sell
- **Rural land or raw land:** Substantial premium or decline — illiquid, value is subjective
- **Partially completed construction:** Highest risk; the collateral has limited market without finishing

### 4. Deal Complexity and Term

Longer terms carry more uncertainty. A 6-month loan has less time for things to go wrong than a 24-month loan. Rate accordingly.

Also consider what happens at maturity. A fix-and-flip with a clear sale exit is lower risk than a bridge loan where the exit depends on the borrower getting approved for permanent financing — an outcome outside anyone's direct control.

### 5. Your Own Capital Costs and Return Targets

You have a minimum return threshold. If you can earn 5–6% in a high-yield savings account with zero risk, your private loan rates need to compensate you adequately for:
- Illiquidity (your capital is locked up)
- Credit risk (borrower default)
- Execution risk (the project doesn't finish on time)
- Administrative burden (origination, servicing, monitoring)

For most lenders, the minimum worthwhile private loan rate in today's environment is 9–10% — below that, the risk/reward math doesn't work. Your target net yield (after defaults, extensions, and dead time between loans) will typically be 1–2 points lower than your stated rate.

## Points and Fee Structure

Interest rate is only part of the equation. Most private lenders also charge origination points — a percentage of the loan amount paid at closing.

A 2-point origination fee on a $150,000 loan is $3,000, paid at closing. This has two effects:

1. It increases your effective yield significantly on short-term loans (2 points on a 6-month loan is the equivalent of an additional 4% annual yield)
2. It creates a meaningful upfront payment that doesn't depend on the loan staying current

**Typical point structures by deal type:**
- Low-risk, repeat borrower, conservative LTV: 1 point
- Standard deal: 1.5–2 points
- Higher risk or first-time borrower: 2–3 points

Points are negotiated. In a market with more lender capital than deal flow, points compress. In a tight lending environment, borrowers accept higher points to get funded. Know your local market.

Some lenders also charge:
- **Extension fees:** If the borrower needs 3 more months, they pay a fee (often 1–2 points) for the extension
- **Draw fees:** For construction loans where capital is disbursed in stages
- **Servicing fees:** If you use a third-party servicer

## A Worked Example

**Deal:** $180,000 ARV residential fix-and-flip. Property purchased for $110,000; borrower needs $40,000 in renovation funds.

**Loan request:** $120,000 total (70% of $110,000 purchase price, plus renovation budget)

Wait — let's think about this differently. The LTV that matters is against the ARV:
- $120,000 loan / $180,000 ARV = 67% LTV ✓

**Risk assessment:**
- LTV: 67% — solid, within normal range
- Borrower: 3 completed flips in the same market — experienced enough
- Property type: SFR, suburban — liquid collateral
- Term: 12 months — standard
- Exit: Sale — clear and reasonable

**Rate decision:**
- Market starting point for this deal type: 11%
- LTV adjustment: 0% (in normal range)
- Borrower experience adjustment: -0.5% (a few deals, not a seasoned veteran)
- Final rate: 10.5%

**Points:** 2 points on $120,000 = $2,400 at closing

**Effective yield:** On a 12-month loan at 10.5% plus 2 points, your effective annualized yield is approximately 12.5% before any drag from dead time or defaults.

Not bad for a loan backed by a property worth $60,000 more than you've lent against it.

## Usury Laws: Know Your Floor and Ceiling

Every state has usury laws — limits on the maximum interest rate that can be charged on loans. The complexity is real:

- Most states have different limits for consumer loans vs. business loans
- Some states exempt loans above a certain size from usury limits
- Some states have no practical cap on business-purpose loans
- The characterization of the borrower (individual vs. LLC) can change which rules apply

**The key distinction:** Most private real estate lending is made to business entities (the borrower's LLC) for a business purpose (real estate investment). These loans are typically exempt from consumer usury protections in most states.

If you're lending to individuals rather than LLCs, you need to be much more careful about usury compliance. Consumer mortgage rates are regulated at both state and federal levels.

**States to pay special attention to:**
- California has complex usury rules but broad exemptions for real estate loans made by licensed lenders and for business-purpose loans
- New York has strict usury limits for some loan categories
- Texas has specific rules for real estate lending

Don't rely on this article for usury compliance. Get a real estate attorney in your state to confirm what rates are permissible for your specific loan types. One hour of legal advice is worth far more than the cost of getting this wrong.

## When to Walk Away

Rate is not the only lever. Sometimes the right answer is to decline the loan entirely — and no rate is high enough to make a bad deal acceptable.

Walk away when:
- The LTV doesn't work even at a lower loan amount
- You can't independently verify the ARV
- The borrower's business plan requires things to go perfectly
- The collateral is illiquid (rural, unusual property type, contamination history)
- The borrower has a prior default or bad reference from another lender
- Your gut says something is off

Private lending returns are made by avoiding bad loans, not by charging higher rates on risky ones. The best lenders are disciplined decliners.

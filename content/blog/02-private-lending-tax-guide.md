# Private Lending Tax Guide: What You Need to Know

Tax season is when private lending gets real. During the year, collecting interest feels straightforward. Then February arrives, your accountant asks for your 1099-INT totals, and you realize you haven't been tracking interest income per borrower — just depositing checks.

This guide covers the tax mechanics of private lending for individual lenders and small LLCs. It's not a substitute for a CPA who understands your situation, but it will get you fluent in the concepts before that conversation.

---

## The Core Principle: Interest Income Is Ordinary Income

Let's start with the most important fact: interest income from private loans is taxed as ordinary income, not capital gains. That means it's taxed at your marginal federal rate — the same rate as your salary, freelance income, or any other earned income.

If you're in the 24% federal bracket and you earn $30,000 in private loan interest this year, you owe roughly $7,200 in federal income tax on that interest (plus state income tax, where applicable). There's no preferential rate, no depreciation offset, no special treatment.

This is the most important tax comparison to understand when evaluating private lending against other asset classes like real estate (which benefits from depreciation) or long-term stocks (taxed at lower capital gains rates). Private lending interest is fully exposed to your ordinary income rate.

---

## What Counts as Interest Income

Interest income from a private loan is taxable when it's received (cash-basis taxpayers) or when it accrues (accrual-basis taxpayers). Most individual lenders use cash-basis accounting, which means you report interest in the year you actually receive the payment.

**Scheduled interest payments.** The interest portion of each monthly payment is income in the year received.

**Points and origination fees.** If you charge a borrower points (a percentage of the loan amount at closing), these are generally taxable in the year received — though the exact treatment can vary based on how the fee is characterized.

**Default interest.** If your promissory note specifies a higher interest rate during default and you collect it, that's also income.

**Late fees.** Taxable as income.

**Prepayment penalties.** If your loan documents include a prepayment penalty and the borrower pays it, that's income.

Keep a clear record of each payment received, with the date and breakdown between principal and interest. ## The 1098: When You're Required to File One

Most private lenders are surprised to learn this: **if the loan is secured by real property and the borrower paid you $600 or more in mortgage interest during the year, you may be required to file a Form 1098 (Mortgage Interest Statement).**

### The Technical Rule

IRS regulations require any "trade or business" that receives $600+ in mortgage interest from an individual during the calendar year to file a 1098 with the IRS and provide a copy to the payer (your borrower).

The pivotal question is whether your private lending activity constitutes a "trade or business." This is a facts-and-circumstances determination. The IRS looks at factors including:

- How many loans you have active
- Whether lending is your primary income source
- How actively you're involved in the business of lending
- Whether you hold yourself out as a lender

**If you're in the business of private lending** (multiple loans, active solicitation, ongoing operations), you likely are required to file 1098s for qualifying loans.

**If you make an occasional loan** (one or two in your lifetime, to people you know), you may not be in a "trade or business" and may not have the 1098 obligation.

The middle ground — which is where many micro-lenders live — is genuinely ambiguous. A CPA who works with real estate investors is the right person to make this call for your situation.

### Who Gets a 1098?

The 1098 obligation applies when:
- The loan is secured by real property (your standard private real estate loan)
- The borrower is an individual (not a corporation or LLC)
- Interest paid during the year was $600 or more

If your borrower is a business entity (an LLC or corporation), you generally don't file a 1098 for that loan. This is the most common situation in private lending — most real estate investors borrow through their LLCs, which means your 1098 obligation is often limited or nonexistent.

### What the 1098 Contains

If you are required to file, the 1098 needs:
- Lender's name, address, and TIN (your SSN or EIN)
- Borrower's name, address, and TIN
- Amount of mortgage interest received
- Outstanding mortgage principal at the start of the year
- Origination date
- Property address

### Filing Deadlines

- Furnish Copy B to the borrower by **January 31**
- File with the IRS by **February 28** (paper) or **March 31** (electronic)

Penalties for failure to file can run $50–$290 per return depending on how late you file and whether the failure was intentional.

## Reporting Your Interest Income

### Schedule B (Interest and Dividend Income)

Interest income from private loans goes on Schedule B of your Form 1040. You list each payer (your borrowers, in this case) and the interest amount received. If you have multiple loans across multiple borrowers, each gets its own line.

This is one reason why per-loan tracking matters. You need to be able to state with confidence, "Borrower A paid me $8,400 in interest in 2025; Borrower B paid me $12,200." That number comes from your payment records.

### Schedule C vs. Schedule E: Which One Is Right?

This is one of the most common questions from new private lenders, and the answer depends on how your lending activity is characterized.

**Schedule C (Profit or Loss from Business)**

Use Schedule C if your private lending qualifies as a trade or business. Schedule C lets you deduct ordinary and necessary business expenses, which is a significant benefit. The downside: Schedule C income is subject to self-employment tax (15.3% on net earnings up to the Social Security wage base) in addition to regular income tax.

Most active private lenders with multiple loans and ongoing deal-finding activity are on Schedule C.

**Schedule E (Supplemental Income and Loss)**

Some lenders report private loan interest on Schedule E, particularly if the lending activity is passive or limited. Schedule E income is not subject to self-employment tax, which is a meaningful savings. However, if the IRS determines you're actually in the business of lending, this characterization can be challenged.

**The LLC question**

If you lend through a single-member LLC (the most common structure), the LLC is a disregarded entity for federal tax purposes. The income and expenses flow through to your personal return as if you held the loans directly. You'd still use Schedule C or Schedule E depending on how the activity is characterized.

A multi-member LLC would file a Form 1065 (Partnership Return) and issue Schedule K-1s to members.

**Get a CPA's opinion** on which schedule is appropriate for your situation. The difference between Schedule C and Schedule E isn't just a reporting question — it affects your tax bill and your risk of audit scrutiny.

## What You Can Deduct

If your lending qualifies as a business (Schedule C), you can deduct ordinary and necessary business expenses.

**Common deductible expenses:**

*Professional fees.* Attorney fees for loan document preparation, CPA fees for tax preparation, and any consulting you pay for underwriting or deal evaluation.

*Software and tools.* Loan management software, financial modeling tools, and any subscriptions you use to run the business.

*Interest expense.* If you've borrowed money (from a HELOC, for example) to fund loans, the interest you pay may be deductible.

*Office expenses.* If you have a dedicated home office or business office, a portion of those costs may be deductible.

*Education and professional development.* Books, courses, and seminars related to private lending.

*Travel.* Property inspection travel, attending investor meetups for deal sourcing, and similar business travel.

*Bad debt.* If a borrower defaults and you write off the loan balance as uncollectible, you may be able to deduct the loss. The rules here are specific — you need to have actually written off the debt as worthless, and it needs to be a legitimate business bad debt rather than a nonbusiness bad debt (which gets capital loss treatment instead).

**What you generally can't deduct:**

Losses on loans that are still outstanding (no deduction until the debt is actually written off), and personal expenses mixed in with business expenses.

Keep receipts. Organize expenses by category. Make your accountant's job easy.

## Quarterly Estimated Taxes

If your private lending generates meaningful income, you're probably required to make quarterly estimated tax payments.

The IRS requires estimated payments if you expect to owe at least $1,000 in federal tax after withholding and credits for the year. For private lenders with consistent monthly interest income, this is almost always triggered.

**2026 Quarterly deadlines:**
- Q1 (Jan 1 – Mar 31): Due April 15
- Q2 (Apr 1 – May 31): Due June 16
- Q3 (Jun 1 – Aug 31): Due September 15
- Q4 (Sep 1 – Dec 31): Due January 15, 2027

The penalty for underpaying is calculated on the underpaid amount for each quarter — it's not devastating, but it's avoidable.

The safe harbor amount: if you pay either 100% of last year's tax liability (110% if your AGI exceeded $150,000), or 90% of this year's actual liability, you'll avoid penalties even if you owe at filing.

For most private lenders, estimating your quarterly payments at roughly 30–35% of your interest income (covering federal income tax plus self-employment tax, if applicable) is a reasonable starting point. Your CPA can calculate the precise number.

## Year-End Checklist

Before you hand off your records to your accountant, pull together:

1. **Per-loan interest received** — the total interest paid to you by each borrower during the year
2. **Loan origination fees or points received** — listed by loan and borrower
3. **Outstanding principal balances** as of December 31 (needed if you file 1098s)
4. **Business expenses** — organized by category with receipts
5. **Any loans paid off during the year** — final payoff amounts and dates
6. **Any loans in default or workout** — your accountant will want to know about exposure that may become a deduction

## A Note on State Taxes

Everything above has focused on federal taxes. Your state likely has its own income tax rules, and some states have additional taxes or requirements for lending businesses (gross receipts taxes, franchise taxes, etc.). A CPA familiar with your state is essential if you're operating at any meaningful scale.

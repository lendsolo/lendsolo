# Is Private Lending Legal in Texas? What You Need to Know

Texas is one of the biggest private lending markets in the United States—and for good reason. Strong population growth, a booming real estate market, and a legal framework that generally favors creditors make it a magnet for private money. Dallas-Fort Worth, Houston, Austin, and San Antonio consistently rank among the top metros for fix-and-flip activity and new construction, which means there's steady demand for non-bank capital.

But "lender-friendly" doesn't mean "anything goes." Texas has a unique set of rules—some written into the state constitution itself—that can catch lenders off guard, especially those coming from other states. Usury violations carry stiff penalties. Homestead protections are among the strongest in the country. And the foreclosure process, while fast, has specific procedural requirements that must be followed to the letter.

This guide covers the key legal rules every private lender operating in Texas should understand. It's not a substitute for legal counsel, but it will give you a solid working knowledge of the landscape so you can structure your deals with confidence.

## Is Private Lending Legal in Texas?

The short answer: yes, absolutely. Texas has a deep tradition of property rights and private contract law. There is no general prohibition against one private party lending money to another, whether or not that loan is secured by real estate.

The critical distinction you need to understand is between **consumer-purpose loans** and **business-purpose loans**. This distinction affects almost everything—what regulations apply, what disclosures are required, and what interest rates you can charge.

A consumer-purpose loan is one where the borrower uses the proceeds for personal, family, or household purposes. Think: a homeowner taking out a loan to renovate their primary residence or consolidate personal debt. These loans trigger a mountain of federal and state consumer protection rules—Truth in Lending Act (TILA), RESPA, Dodd-Frank ability-to-repay requirements, and more.

A business-purpose loan is one where the borrower uses the proceeds for a business or investment activity. A borrower taking out a loan to buy and flip a rental property, fund new construction, or bridge to a refinance on an investment property is making a business-purpose loan. These loans are exempt from most federal consumer lending regulations.

The vast majority of private real estate loans—fix-and-flip, bridge, rental acquisition, new construction—are business-purpose loans. That's the lane most solo private lenders operate in, and it's where Texas law gives you the most room to work.

That said, the exemption isn't automatic. You need to document the business purpose properly. Have the borrower sign a business-purpose affidavit at closing, and make sure your loan documents clearly state the loan's purpose. If a dispute ever arises, you want a clear paper trail showing this was not a consumer transaction.

## Texas Usury Laws and Rate Limits

Texas takes usury seriously. The rules are rooted in the Texas Constitution itself—specifically Article XVI, Section 11—which means they can't be changed by simple legislation. Understanding these limits is non-negotiable.

Here's how it breaks down:

### The Constitutional Baseline

The Texas Constitution itself sets the default usury limit at **10% per annum**. Any contract charging more than 10% is deemed usurious unless a higher rate is specifically authorized by statute.

For loans with no written agreement specifying the rate, the default maximum is **6% per annum**.

### The Statutory Ceiling

The Texas Finance Code, Section 303.009, raises the general maximum to **18% per annum** for most written contracts where the parties have agreed to a specific rate. This is the ceiling that applies to most private lending transactions that don't qualify for one of the higher alternative ceilings described below.

### Business-Purpose and Commercial Loans

This is where it gets more favorable for private lenders. Texas law provides several alternative ceilings that allow higher rates for specific loan types:

- **Commercial loans over $250,000**: Under Texas Finance Code Chapter 306, "qualified commercial loans" — which include business-purpose real estate loans with principal exceeding $250,000 — benefit from higher rate ceilings. The standard 18% ceiling can float upward to **24% or even 28%** depending on the loan amount and market conditions. These loans also get expanded flexibility around equity participation, fee structures, and prepayment premiums. This is not an unlimited exemption — rate ceilings still apply — but they are significantly higher than the general 18% cap.
- **Loans to business entities**: Loans made to corporations, LLCs, and partnerships for business purposes also benefit from the expanded commercial loan provisions under Chapter 306.
- **The "indicated rate ceiling"**: This is a floating ceiling tied to 18% per annum or the weekly rate published by the Consumer Credit Commissioner, whichever is greater. It rarely matters in practice because most private real estate loans exceed the $250,000 threshold or qualify for other exemptions.

### What Happens if You Violate the Usury Limit

This is where Texas law has real teeth. If a lender charges a usurious rate, the penalties differ based on the loan type but are severe in all cases:

- **Commercial loans**: The borrower can recover **three times the excess interest** — that is, three times the difference between what you charged and the maximum rate allowed by law — plus reasonable attorney's fees (Texas Finance Code Section 305.001(a-1)).
- **Consumer loans**: The penalties are even harsher. The borrower can recover three times the excess interest, plus attorney's fees. And if you charge more than **double** the authorized rate on a consumer loan, you forfeit the **entire principal, all interest, and all other charges** (Section 305.002). This is effectively a total wipeout.

These aren't theoretical penalties. Texas courts enforce them, and the treble damages calculation can add up fast. Even on a commercial business-purpose loan, a borrower who proves usury can recover a substantial judgment against you — and you'll pay their legal costs on top of it.

The practical takeaway: if your loan is under $250,000 and made to an individual (not a business entity), stay at or below 18%. If you're above that threshold or lending to an entity for business purposes, you have more room — but you still face rate ceilings, and the penalties for crossing them are steep. Document everything carefully and have your fee structure reviewed by an attorney.

## Licensing Requirements (or Lack Thereof)

One of the things that makes Texas attractive for private lenders is the licensing landscape—or rather, the lack of one for most solo lenders.

### When You Don't Need a License

If you are lending your own funds for business-purpose real estate loans, Texas does not require you to hold a lending license. This applies whether you're making one loan a year or twenty. The key factors are:

1. You're using your own capital (not pooling funds from other investors).
2. The loans are business-purpose, not consumer-purpose.
3. You're acting as the lender, not as a broker arranging loans between other parties.

This is a major advantage. Many states require mortgage lending licenses even for private lenders making a handful of loans per year. Texas doesn't—as long as you stay in your lane.

### When You DO Need a License

Licensing requirements kick in when you step outside the solo-lender-using-own-funds model:

- **Brokering loans**: If you're arranging loans between borrowers and other lenders (even other private individuals), you may need a mortgage broker license from the Texas Department of Savings and Mortgage Lending (DSML) or registration with the Nationwide Multistate Licensing System (NMLS).
- **Consumer-purpose lending**: If you make consumer-purpose mortgage loans (loans secured by the borrower's primary residence for personal use), you're subject to the SAFE Act and need an NMLS-registered mortgage loan originator.
- **Fund management and investor pooling**: If you're raising capital from multiple investors to fund loans, you may be creating a security, which triggers SEC and Texas State Securities Board registration requirements. This is a common issue when lenders grow and start managing other people's money.

The Texas Office of Consumer Credit Commissioner (OCCC) regulates certain types of consumer lending, including some mortgage lending activities. If you're unsure whether your activities cross into regulated territory, the OCCC's website is a reasonable starting point—but talk to an attorney before making assumptions.

## Lien Priority and Recording

Getting your lien recorded properly is fundamental. In Texas, lien priority generally follows the "first in time, first in right" rule—whoever records their deed of trust first has the senior lien position.

### Recording Your Deed of Trust

Texas uses a deed of trust structure (not a mortgage) for real estate liens. The deed of trust must be recorded with the county clerk in the county where the property is located. Recording is what gives you notice priority—without it, a subsequent lender could record their lien ahead of yours and jump your position in line.

There is no statutory deadline for recording, but every day you wait is a day another lien could jump ahead of you. Best practice: record the same day as closing or the next business day.

### Title Insurance

Always get a lender's title insurance policy. A title search will reveal existing liens, judgments, and encumbrances, but title insurance protects you against things the search might miss—forged documents, undisclosed heirs, recording errors.

The cost is modest relative to the protection. On a $250,000 loan in Texas, expect to pay roughly $1,500–$2,000 for a lender's title policy. It's one of the cheapest forms of risk mitigation in your toolkit.

### Texas Homestead Protections

This is the one that surprises out-of-state lenders. Texas has some of the most aggressive homestead protections in the country. Under the Texas Constitution, a homestead is protected from forced sale for most debts. You cannot foreclose on a borrower's homestead to collect a general debt.

There are limited exceptions—purchase money mortgages, property tax liens, home equity loans (which have their own strict set of rules under Article XVI, Section 50(a)(6) of the Texas Constitution), and mechanic's liens under certain conditions.

The practical implication: if your borrower claims the property as their homestead, your ability to foreclose may be severely limited unless your loan fits within one of the narrow exceptions. This is why most private lenders focus on investment properties, not owner-occupied homes. Verify occupancy status before funding any deal.

## Foreclosure Process in Texas

Texas is a **non-judicial foreclosure state**, which means lenders can foreclose without going through the courts. This is one of the primary reasons Texas is popular for hard money and private lending—it makes the foreclosure process faster, cheaper, and more predictable than in judicial foreclosure states like New York or New Jersey.

### How It Works

The standard foreclosure process in Texas follows this timeline:

1. **Default occurs**: The borrower misses payments or otherwise violates the loan terms.
2. **Notice of default and intent to accelerate**: You must send the borrower written notice that the loan is in default and that you intend to accelerate (demand full repayment). The borrower gets at least **20 days** to cure the default.
3. **Notice of foreclosure sale**: If the borrower doesn't cure, you file a notice of sale with the county clerk and send it to the borrower at least **21 days** before the sale date.
4. **Foreclosure auction**: Sales happen on the **first Tuesday of each month** between 10:00 AM and 4:00 PM at the county courthouse (or the area designated by the county commissioners). This is not flexible—if you miss the first Tuesday, you wait until the next month.

The entire process, from first notice to sale, can be completed in roughly **60 days** under optimal conditions. Compare that to 12–18 months or more in judicial foreclosure states.

### Important Details

- The sale must take place in the county where the property is located.
- You (the lender) can bid at the foreclosure auction—and often should, to protect your position if no third-party buyers appear.
- Texas requires that the foreclosure sale price be "fair market value" for deficiency judgment purposes (if you want to pursue the borrower for the difference between the sale price and the debt).
- After the sale, there is **no statutory right of redemption** for the borrower on most non-homestead properties. Once the sale is complete, it's done.

## Common Mistakes Texas Private Lenders Make

Even experienced lenders get tripped up in Texas. Here are the most common errors:

### Not Recording the Deed of Trust Promptly

This seems obvious, but it happens more often than you'd think—especially when closings are done informally or without a title company. Every day your deed of trust sits unrecorded is a day your lien position is at risk. Use a title company, and confirm recording within 48 hours of closing.

### Ignoring Homestead Protections

Lending against a property without verifying whether it's the borrower's homestead is a serious mistake. If the borrower later claims homestead protection, you may find your deed of trust is unenforceable. Always conduct a thorough occupancy check, and include representations and warranties about non-homestead status in your loan documents.

### Accidentally Charging Usurious Interest

This tends to happen when lenders stack fees—origination points, late fees, default interest, extension fees—without realizing that some of these can be classified as "interest" under Texas law. Texas courts look at the substance of the charges, not just the label. If your combined charges push the effective rate above the applicable ceiling, you've committed usury. Have your fee structure reviewed by an attorney.

### Skipping the Promissory Note or Using a Template

A deed of trust without a properly drafted promissory note is a recipe for problems. The promissory note is the document that creates the actual debt obligation—the deed of trust just secures it. And using a generic template downloaded from the internet, without having it reviewed by a Texas attorney, often means missing Texas-specific requirements. The few hundred dollars you spend on proper legal documents is trivial compared to the cost of an unenforceable note.

## Conclusion

Texas is, by most measures, one of the best states in the country for private lending. The combination of no licensing requirement for solo lenders using their own funds, a fast non-judicial foreclosure process, and a strong real estate market makes it an ideal operating environment.

But "friendly" doesn't mean "simple." The usury rules have constitutional teeth. Homestead protections can make an otherwise solid loan unenforceable. And the procedural requirements for foreclosure, while straightforward, must be followed precisely.

Understand these rules, document your loans properly, and work with a Texas real estate attorney who knows private lending. That's the foundation for building a profitable lending business in the Lone Star State.

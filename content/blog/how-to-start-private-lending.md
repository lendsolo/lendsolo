## Why Private Lending?

Private lending — sometimes called hard money lending or peer-to-peer lending — is one of the most accessible ways to generate passive income from capital you already have. Unlike stocks or real estate, private lending gives you a fixed return schedule, monthly cash flow, and a clearly defined exit date.

The concept is simple: you lend money to a borrower at an agreed interest rate and term, secured by collateral (usually real estate), and they pay you back with interest. If they don't, you have a legal claim on the collateral.

But "simple" doesn't mean "easy." Most new private lenders make mistakes in their first few deals that cost them months of headaches — or worse, real money. This guide covers the fundamentals.

## Setting Your Lending Criteria

Before you fund a single deal, you need to define your lending box. This is the set of criteria a deal must meet before you'll even consider it:

- **Loan size range:** What's the minimum and maximum you'll lend? Most solo lenders start between $25,000 and $150,000.
- **Interest rate floor:** What's the lowest rate you'll accept? For private loans, 8–15% annual is typical depending on risk.
- **Term limits:** How long are you comfortable having your capital tied up? 6–24 months is standard for bridge loans.
- **Collateral requirements:** Will you lend unsecured, or do you require real estate, equipment, or other hard assets?
- **Geographic focus:** Lending laws vary by state. Start in jurisdictions you understand.

Write these down. Having criteria in advance prevents you from talking yourself into bad deals when a borrower is persuasive.

## Structuring Your First Deal

Every private loan needs a promissory note at minimum. This is the legally binding document that establishes:

1. The principal amount
2. The interest rate and how it's calculated (simple vs. compound, annual vs. monthly)
3. The payment schedule (monthly, interest-only, balloon, etc.)
4. The maturity date
5. Late payment terms and default provisions
6. Prepayment terms

If the loan is secured by real estate, you'll also need a deed of trust or mortgage document, recorded with the county. This is what gives you the legal right to foreclose if the borrower defaults.

**Do not skip the paperwork.** Many new lenders — especially those lending to friends or family — skip formal documentation. This is the single most common mistake and the hardest to recover from.

## Understanding Loan Types

As a private lender, you'll encounter several common structures:

### Fully Amortizing Loans
The borrower pays the same amount each month, with each payment covering both interest and principal. By the end of the term, the loan is fully paid off. This is the safest structure for lenders because you're getting principal back throughout the life of the loan.

### Interest-Only Loans
The borrower pays only interest each month, with the full principal due as a balloon payment at maturity. These are common for short-term bridge loans and real estate rehab financing. Higher risk because your entire principal is at stake until the final payment.

### Balloon Loans
Similar to interest-only, but may include partial principal payments. The key feature is a large lump-sum payment due at maturity.

## Managing Risk

Risk management in private lending comes down to three things:

### 1. Collateral Coverage
The loan-to-value (LTV) ratio is your most important risk metric. If you're lending $100,000 against a property worth $150,000, your LTV is 67%. Most experienced private lenders stay below 70% LTV. Below 65% is conservative and ideal for your first deals.

### 2. Portfolio Concentration
Never put more than 25–30% of your total lending capital into a single deal. If that one borrower defaults, you need the rest of your portfolio generating income while you work through the recovery process.

### 3. Documentation
Document everything. Every payment received, every communication with the borrower, every modification to the original terms. If a deal goes sideways, your documentation is your defense.

## Tax Reporting

Interest income from private lending is taxable as ordinary income. You'll need to:

- Track total interest received per borrower per year
- Issue 1099-INT forms to borrowers who paid you more than $10 in interest
- Separate principal repayments (not taxable) from interest payments (taxable)
- Report on Schedule B of your personal tax return

This is where most spreadsheet-based lenders run into trouble. Manually separating principal from interest across multiple loans with different terms and payment histories is tedious and error-prone. One wrong formula and your tax filing is incorrect.

## Getting Started

1. **Define your lending criteria** before you look at any deals
2. **Consult a real estate attorney** in your state to understand local lending regulations
3. **Start with one loan** — don't try to build a portfolio overnight
4. **Use proper documentation** — promissory note, deed of trust, and a clear payment schedule
5. **Track everything** from day one — payments, communications, and modifications

Private lending rewards discipline and preparation. The lenders who succeed long-term are the ones who treat it like a business from the start, not a casual side arrangement.
